# Can't Get You Out of My Head: Facts & Connections

### Adam Curtis (2021, BBC) — An Emotional History of the Modern World

> *"These strange days did not just happen — we, and those in power, created them together."*

This document maps the most surprising facts, hidden connections, and controversial claims across all six episodes of Adam Curtis's documentary series. The connections are organised thematically, not by episode, to show the web that Curtis weaves across time, geography, and ideology.

## The Web of Connections

### 1. The Boole Family Tree
**George Boole → Boolean Logic → Ethel Boole → The Gadfly → Chinese Revolution → Geoffrey Hinton → Neural Networks → Google**

- **George Boole**, a deeply religious mathematician from Doncaster, had a vision in the 1840s while walking across a field: he saw how mathematics could map human thought in binary form — *if this, then that; if that, then not this*. He published *An Investigation of the Laws of Thought* (1854).
- Bertrand Russell admired Boole's mathematics but warned: *"Human beings do not think like that."* What Boole was really doing was creating an abstract system to catalogue and control — the intellectual equivalent of the Empire's maps of its "dark interior."
- Boolean logic became the foundation of all computer algorithms — the binary switching (0 or 1) that drives every digital machine.
- Boole's daughter **Ethel Boole** went to Russia, became involved with revolutionaries in St Petersburg, and wrote a novel called *The Gadfly* about a young woman who sacrifices everything for revolution.
- Without her knowledge, *The Gadfly* inspired millions of revolutionaries — first in Russia in the 1920s, then in China, where young fighters carried it in their backpacks during the revolution.
- Ethel married a Polish revolutionary called **Wilfrid Voynich**. She inherited the **Voynich Manuscript** — a mysterious book written in a language no one has ever been able to decipher.
- When the Bolshoi Ballet visited New York in 1959, the dancers were astonished to find Ethel alive and rushed to visit her — she had no idea she was a hero of the Russian Revolution.
- Boole's great-great-grandson was **Geoffrey Hinton**, the psychologist who solved the problem of artificial intelligence. In the 1990s, Hinton realised that feeding rules of human logic into machines had failed. Instead, he did the opposite: feed mass data into neural networks and let the machines find their own patterns.
- Hinton began working at **Google** and called what he created **Vector-World** — machines that cross back and forth across time and space looking for links and patterns that humans would never see or understand.
- When the Bolshoi visited Ethel in New York in 1959, she was living quietly, completely forgotten. She had no idea she was a hero of two revolutions.
- The chain: a Victorian religious vision about thought → the logic of empire → the foundations of computing → a revolutionary novel → the Chinese Revolution → AI that sees a hidden reality humans cannot access.

### 2. The Opium Circle
**British opium trade → China's humiliation → Deng's revenge via cheap goods → American factory closures → OxyContin → The Sackler Family**

- In the 19th century, the British forced opium onto China, poisoning and corrupting millions. In return, Britain received a vast wave of silver that made it the richest country in the world. One historian wrote: *"Deeply involved as it was in one of the most pernicious, yet well-organised and profitable drug trades that has ever existed, the British Empire was rotten at its heart."*
- In the 1870s, the **Society for the Suppression of the Opium Trade** was formed. Its pamphlets about the horrors in China caused a sensation and hundreds of thousands joined. But as the campaign grew, a strange thing happened — the guilt mutated into fear.
- The guilt over opium mutated into fear — the **Yellow Peril**. Despite fewer than 1,000 Chinese people living in Britain, a hysteria swept the country about Chinese-run opium dens and a coming revenge. The panic also spread through America, especially the west coast where there were Chinese migrant workers. This culminated in the invention of **Dr Fu Manchu** — *"a yellow Satan, an archangel of evil who wants to take over the whole world."*
- After Tiananmen Square, **Deng Xiaoping** reawakened this history as a propaganda tool — the "Century of Humiliation" narrative. He commissioned a multimillion-dollar film about the British opium invasion, complete with British actors, and even an early computer game where you could fight the British invaders.
- Deng's real revenge: use the mass mobilisation of the Chinese people to create goods so cheap that everyone in the West would want them — *"the equivalent of opium."* Whole cities were built that made just one kind of product.
- As Chinese factories grew, **American factories closed**. Workers who were laid off discovered they got more benefits if they were disabled, so they told doctors they were injured. The doctors gave them **OxyContin** — a synthetic form of opium made by the company founded by **Arthur Sackler**.
- Sackler had earlier marketed **Valium** in the 1970s to treat suburban anxiety and loneliness, promoting it as non-addictive. The First Lady **Betty Ford** publicly revealed her Valium addiction in 1978, exposing *"a private, hidden world of anxiety behind the public faces that affected millions."*
- OxyContin made people feel *"safe, in a bubble, protected from the anxieties and fears of the new post-industrial world."*
- **Jiang Zemin** instructed that all dollars China got from exporting goods should be sent back to America to buy up US Government debt. This made the dollar rise (keeping Chinese goods cheap) and kept US interest rates low (so Americans borrowed and bought even more). The Chinese created *"a safe bubble, wrapped around the United States"* — but in the process created *"the biggest consumer and property boom ever in history."*
- The Chinese money insulated Americans from the Iraq War — no economic impact, no protests, cost nearly $1 billion per day but no pressure on politicians. *"Safe in their protected bubble, few of the American people protested."*
- The circle: Britain poisons China with opium → China uses cheap goods to flood the West → American workers lose jobs → they are prescribed a synthetic opium made by the same family that pushed Valium → China buys US debt creating a consumption bubble → history repeats as pharmacological and financial control.

### 3. Operation Mindfuck
**Discordianism → Fake Illuminati → Real conspiracies → QAnon / Online conspiracy culture → Tupac's Killuminati**

- In the 1960s, **Kerry Thornley** co-founded **Discordianism**, a movement against all politics and systems of power. He wanted to free individuals from authoritarian conditioning.
- Thornley had been a Marine with **Lee Harvey Oswald** and had written a novel about him called *The Idle Warriors*. He later lived in New Orleans — the same city Oswald had lived in before the assassination. District Attorney **Jim Garrison** accused Thornley of being part of the JFK conspiracy.
- Garrison's method — **Time and Propinquity** — said: don't look for meaning, look for patterns, strange coincidences, and links. This method would profoundly shape how millions of people came to understand the world.
- In 1969, Thornley launched **Operation Mindfuck**: he and **Greg Hill** planted a fake letter in Playboy (between letters about gun fanatics' penises and heavy petting) claiming that the **Illuminati** were behind all political assassinations.
- Their aim was to make conspiracy theories look absurd — no one could possibly believe an 18th-century Bavarian organisation was secretly ruling the modern world. *It was clearly ridiculous.*
- But then real conspiracies emerged (Watergate, CIA assassinations, MKUltra), and the fake Illuminati stories got mixed up with the real ones. The line between political reality and conspiracy dreamworld blurred.
- Thornley himself fell victim. After seeing a photo of a Watergate burglar he thought he'd met in New Orleans, he became convinced the CIA had somehow manipulated him into starting Operation Mindfuck — but he had no idea why. He retreated into his own conspiracy theory.
- His friend **Greg Hill** became an alcoholic and wrote: *"It is not injustice that overwhelms me now, but my sheer damn inability to know anything with any deep level of certainty. When despair is deep enough, even death seems pointless."*
- Decades later, the online world mixed Operation Mindfuck's Illuminati stories with MKUltra, Disney, and celebrity culture. Millions became convinced that stars from Britney Spears to Beyonce were controlled by the Illuminati, who hid messages (especially triangles) in music videos.
- **Tupac Shakur** anticipated this. His posthumous album **Killuminati** attacked the retreat into Illuminati conspiracy theories, arguing they were a way of *avoiding* confronting the real powers that controlled people's lives. His message: *"Suspicion is just another form of control."*
- **QAnon** emerged from the same ecosystem — a conspiracy theory that Trump was being stopped by a secret cabal of paedophiles. Curtis argues it allowed Trump supporters to avoid facing the fact that he was doing nothing about corruption, while liberals used their own conspiracy theories (Russia, Cambridge Analytica) to avoid facing the genuine grievances behind Trump's election.

### 4. The Surveillance Thread
**Boole's binary logic → Imperial mapping → MKUltra → Horst Herold's electronic surveillance → Google → China's social credit**

- George Boole's logic wasn't about thought — it was about **control**. Throughout the British Empire, science's role was to *"create abstract systems, to catalogue and order the chaotic reality that the British ruled over, to turn it into something that could be managed and controlled"* — from maps of "the dark interior" to cataloguing species and categorising human types.
- In Kenya, the British fought the **Mau Mau** liberation movement by putting hundreds of thousands of Kenyans into special camps for *"psychological adjustment."* Their chief psychologist aimed to manipulate *"the African mind."* What happened turned into *"a frenzied madness"* — mass torture and killing as the British desperately tried to hold on to power. The government in London denied all accusations.
- **Anthony Blunt**, Surveyor of the Queen's Pictures, was asked by MI5 if he was spying for the Russians. He said yes. To avoid embarrassing the Queen, they gave him total immunity and let him carry on working at Buckingham Palace.
- **John le Carré** worked as a spy for MI6. The experience revealed to him what the secret world was really about: *"in a country whose power had collapsed, leaving only a drab, decaying reality, the spies had managed to recreate a magical world where they could go anywhere they wanted, bug, burgle, and even assassinate people without any fear of judgment or control — just like in the Empire."* He described the experience as having *"a voluptuous quality... a necessary sacrifice of morality."*
- The **CIA's MKUltra** programme (1950s–60s), led by psychiatrist **Ewen Cameron** at the Allan Memorial hospital in Montreal, experimented on patients without consent to see if they could wipe and reprogram minds. They used electroshocks and massive LSD doses. Cameron totally failed to implant new memories; his patients were left in a world without meaning. One said: *"I felt like an alien from another world visiting this world."*
- In Greenland, the US military built **Camp Century** — presented as a scientific base but actually hiding **600 nuclear missiles** in tunnels under the ice (**Project Iceworm**). When the base was eventually closed, scientists drilling **ice cores** discovered something unexpected: layers showing the world's temperature had shifted by **ten degrees in just a few centuries** — evidence that climate could change far more violently than anyone imagined. This led **Edward Lorenz** to discover **Chaos Theory** by accident — a rounding error in his climate model produced wildly different results, proving tiny changes could create massive unpredictable consequences.
- In 1970s West Germany, **Horst Herold** invented mass electronic surveillance to catch the Red Army Faction. He created a giant computer network monitoring millions, using what he called *"logical sequences"* — patterns drawn from data predicting where terrorists might be hiding. He explicitly warned: if you don't reform the system, you will need global surveillance to control the anger.
- After 9/11, the **Patriot Act** made everyone's data open to examination. Google's invention — looking for patterns in mass data — became central to national security. The threat of laws stopping Google's data collection *"faded away."*
- Google's shift: originally they dreamed of giving people data to build their own stories. After 9/11 and the dot-com crash, they began **taking** people's data to predict behaviour without asking them anything. *"What they thought or felt as individuals and the stories they told themselves was completely irrelevant."*
- Products designed to mine behaviour data proliferated: **Roomba** models would transmit data about the inside of people's homes; the **We-Vibe vibrator** was discovered transmitting data about intimate behaviour; the German government ordered parents to destroy **Cayla dolls** because they contained *"a concealed espionage device"*; **Pokemon GO** was described as *"herding people to monetised checkpoints without them being fully aware of it."*
- In China, **Xi Jinping** created **Algorithmic Governance** / **Social Credit**: data from all sources tracked whether people bought the right food, didn't cheat at games, didn't jaywalk. Good behaviour earned rewards (discounts, dating site visibility, skipping queues). The aim: make what goes on inside people's heads *"completely irrelevant."*
- The chain: Boolean logic as imperial control → physical surveillance of subjects → psychological reprogramming (MKUltra) → electronic surveillance (Herold) → digital surveillance (Google/NSA) → total behavioural management (China's social credit).

### 5. Revolution Eating Itself
**German anti-fascists at a pro-Hitler Palestinian camp → Entebbe → Black Panthers infiltrated by police → Michael X → Jiang Qing**

- **Horst Mahler** and other German left-wing revolutionaries went to train at a Palestinian camp in Jordan. Things went wrong immediately: **Andreas Baader** insisted on mixed sleeping quarters; the women sunbathed on the roof; Baader declared *"Fucking and shooting are the same thing."*
- Then Mahler discovered the Palestinian fighters had pictures of **Hitler** in the camp. They pointed at them and said *"Good man"* because he had killed the Jews. *"That was very hard for us,"* Mahler said, *"because we had a feeling of guilt about the Jews."*
- The Germans went on strike because they weren't getting enough bullets. The commander threw them out.
- The Palestinian connection led to the **Entebbe hijacking** (1976) — German revolutionaries helped Palestinians hijack a plane and separated Israeli from non-Israeli passengers. *"It seemed as if those dreams had now led them to behave exactly as the Nazis had done."* Curtis frames this as the ultimate self-destruction of the New Left: anti-fascists reproducing fascist methods.
- **Afeni Shakur** (Tupac's mother) was a Black Panther in New York. She suspected one member of her cell, **Yedwa Sudan** (real name Ralph White), was an undercover policeman. When confronted, Sudan pulled a gun, fired two bullets into the table, and accused Shakur of being *"a typical emotional woman."* The group believed him, not her.
- In the **Panther 21 trial**, it emerged that three of the founding members of the cell were undercover police — and they had been the most active members. Afeni Shakur defended herself in court and got the undercover agent to admit that many of the plots had been driven by the police themselves. All 21 were acquitted.
- **Michael de Freitas** (Michael X) worked for the notorious landlord **Peter Rachman** in Notting Hill. Rachman — a Polish Jew who'd been forcibly sterilised by the Nazis, survived Russian labour camps where people killed and ate each other, and fought with the Free Polish Army — was hated as the "face of evil." But de Freitas saw something else: Rachman was the only person giving immigrants and prostitutes somewhere to live, exposing *"the hypocrisy of the nice people at the top of English society."*
- **Edgar Mittelholzer**, a novelist from British Guiana, wrote *My Bones and My Flute* about colonial ghosts that haunted the living. Every evening he listened to Wagner. Then he poured paraffin over himself and burned himself to death — *"a colonial ghost himself, unable to escape the past."*
- **Stokely Carmichael** was the first person to articulate **"Black Power"** — and it happened on a march when **Martin Luther King was absent**. The phrase electrified a generation but terrified white America.
- When **Michael X** was arrested and jailed for 10 months for inciting racial hatred, **Enoch Powell** had made an equally violent anti-immigrant speech — but wasn't charged. The contrast exposed the real power dynamics beneath British liberalism.
- White students arrived in Notting Hill to survey the black community — *"the beginning of community action."* They gathered data, created maps and reports. But what looked like empowerment was really management: the professionals used the data to make themselves indispensable while the black residents they surveyed *"resigned themselves to being observed."*
- De Freitas identified a deep cultural force he called **"Englishism"** — *"an anger and a melancholy at the loss of their empire"* hidden behind the polite veneer of the middle classes.
- Michael X's revolution collapsed into extortion and drug dealing. He persuaded **John Lennon and Yoko Ono** to donate their shaved hair and a large amount of money. He pocketed all of it. He fled to Trinidad, murdered a follower (Gale Benson, daughter of a Conservative MP — stabbed and buried in the garden; the code phrase: *"The tree is planted"*), and was eventually executed.
- **Jiang Qing**, Mao's wife, used the Cultural Revolution to take revenge on everyone who had held her film career back in 1930s Shanghai. Studio heads, directors, and journalists were beaten. She saved the worst for **Li Lili**, a film star she believed had upstaged her in the 1934 film *Bloodshed on Wolf Mountain*. Li Lili was tortured; her husband committed suicide.
- Mao was simply using Jiang Qing and the violence she created to destroy his rivals. He publicly humiliated her: *"You are someone who has grandiose aims but puny abilities, great ambition but little talent."* After his death she was arrested and placed in an underground cell next to the refrigeration unit holding Mao's body. She tried to kill herself by hitting her head against the wall; soldiers covered the walls with rubber.
- In solitary confinement for 10 years, she hoarded socks and handkerchiefs until she had enough to make a rope. In 1991, she hanged herself, leaving a note: *"Today the revolution has been stolen by a clique led by Deng Xiaoping... Chairman, your student and fighter is coming to see you."*

### 6. The Iraq Feedback Loop
**Cecil Sharp's folk revival → Gertrude Bell → Tribal Iraq → Saddam/CIA → Invasion → "Awakening" → ISIS**

- **Cecil Sharp** travelled through England recording old songs and filming folk dances, explicitly as a political project: to create *"a new kind of English nationalism which had, at its heart, the idea of the folk"* — taken from German nationalism. His vision was of an England before mass democracy, where villagers lived in harmony under the lord of the manor.
- The first **Glastonbury Festival** ran from 1914 to 1927, organised by **Rutland Boughton** as the centre of this new English culture. His opera *The Immortal Hour* became a national sensation — set in a dark, mysterious wood with ancient forces called **"the lordly ones."**
- After WWI, upper-class English officials were sent to Baghdad to create Iraq. **Gertrude Bell** and others couldn't afford to survey the country, so they projected onto Iraq the romantic dream of old England: the urban middle classes were "corrupt and untrustworthy"; the tribal sheiks were the "true Iraq" because they represented a natural aristocratic order. Bell said: *"The sheiks are like great aristocrats. They will maintain a natural equilibrium."*
- The truth: the sheiks were marginal figures, and the Ottomans had built a modern, progressive society in the cities. The British tore that apart, then in 1932, facing economic crisis, *"packed up and went, leaving behind a completely unreal and unstable society."*
- Meanwhile, **Hans Morgenthau** (senior US State Department official) named America's hidden system of power the **Dual State**: publicly promoting democracy while secretly overthrowing governments. The CIA ran covert operations against **66 foreign governments**, succeeding in 26 — including Italy, Greece, Syria, Iran, Guatemala, South Vietnam, Indonesia, and Chile. Morgenthau warned this secrecy was *"creating a dangerous time bomb at the heart of America."*
- In 1961, the CIA killed **Patrice Lumumba** in the Congo because the copper in the country's mines was crucial to the new electronics and computers the Americans were building. They installed **Colonel Mobutu** — a brutal dictator they would support for 30 years. *"Rather than establishing a democracy, what the Americans had found themselves doing was continuing the instability created by the old European empires."*
- In 1963, **CIA agents** came to Baghdad. The **Ba'ath Party** mounted a coup. American agents supported it and gave a young **Saddam Hussein** a list of communists in Iraq. Saddam used it to execute thousands, beginning his rise to power.
- Saddam later ordered a feature film about his heroic role in the coup. It was directed by **Terence Young** — who had also directed the James Bond film *Dr No*.
- In 2003, six historians came to see Tony Blair before the invasion. They explained the complex reality of Iraq's groups. There was silence. Blair said: *"Saddam Hussein is an evil man who needs to be removed."* The historians left.
- By 2007, the war cost nearly $1 billion per day. In desperation, the Americans turned to **Sheikh Abdul Sattar Abu Risha** and the tribal system — repeating exactly what the British had done 80 years before.
- The **Awakening** worked temporarily. When American troops left and money stopped, the tribal leaders made a tactical decision: they allied with the very jihadists they'd been fighting — al-Qaida in Iraq. Out of that alliance came **ISIS**.
- *"What had begun a long time ago as a make-believe version of England created in the deserts of Mesopotamia as the British Empire fell apart, had now turned into a terrifying nightmare."*

### 7. Skinner's Pigeons to Social Credit
**Behaviourism → Nudge theory → Facebook experiments → China's algorithmic governance**

- In the 1960s, psychologist **Dr Eckhard Hess** discovered that the pupils in people's eyes react dramatically to different images — changes that come from within, not from light. He believed this gave you a way to see hidden emotional responses the individual might not even be aware of: *"It is almost as though they were a piece of the brain sticking out."*
- **Daniel Kahneman**, inspired by a childhood encounter where an SS officer picked him up and kissed him instead of sending him to the death camps, spent decades studying the brain. He concluded that the "self" is actually a small accessory that simplifies a chaotic mass of biochemical data into stories — stories so simplified they *"bear little relationship to the reality outside."*
- **BF Skinner** showed he could alter pigeons' behaviour through a simple system of rewards — **Operant Conditioning**. *"You can get enormous amounts of behaviour out of these organisms for very little pay."* His novel *Walden Two* described a utopia where all behaviour would be controlled through conditioning.
- In 2014, **Facebook psychologists** announced they could put hidden messages into newsfeeds to create specific moods without users being aware of it — the fusion of nudge theory with technology at industrial scale.
- But then: psychology researchers tried to replicate foundational priming experiments and repeatedly failed. Kahneman himself wrote an open letter warning of *"a train wreck looming"* for the field.
- Curtis's argument: companies like **Cambridge Analytica** may have been exploiting hysteria rather than actually manipulating behaviour. *"You might be able to keep millions of people in a state of constant anxiety online... but you couldn't alter underneath — what they thought and what they believed. People might be far stronger than the scientists believed."*
- China's **Social Credit** system is Skinner's pigeons at national scale: data determines rewards and punishments. *"It was being used to create a whole society where what went on inside people's heads was completely irrelevant."*

### 8. The Emptiness of Individualism
**Nixon's mirror → Valium → Betty Ford → Crack → OxyContin → "You were free but you were alone"**

- **Richard Nixon** told a psychiatrist that when he looked in the mirror in the morning, *"it was as if there was no-one there."*
- Nixon promised to represent the *"silent majority"* — millions who felt isolated and fearful. But he was also uncertain, paranoid, and suspicious. He set up an *"enemies list"* that included journalists, academics, and film stars.
- When Nixon let go of the gold standard in 1971, there was suddenly no fixed value for any currency anywhere in the world. The bankers realised currencies could be traded globally — creating *"a fluid, constantly-changing reality that no-one was fully in control of."*
- **Betty Ford's** revelation of Valium addiction (1978) exposed a hidden epidemic of anxiety across America.
- As factories closed from Chinese competition, crack swept through black communities. **Afeni Shakur** became addicted, and her son **Tupac** was left alone. He said: *"Fear is stronger than love. All the love I gave did me nothing when it came to fear."*
- **Neuroscientist Michael Gazzaniga** studied split-brain patients and argued that there are multiple selves inside the brain. The conscious self constantly invents stories to explain what the other selves are doing. Patient **Vicki** described how one hand would reach into her closet and grab clothes she didn't want — she once found herself putting shorts on top of shorts.
- In 1967, cosmonaut **Vladimir Komarov** was sent into orbit in **Soyuz 1** despite **Yuri Gagarin** having inspected the spacecraft and found hundreds of faults. Gagarin begged them not to launch, but the Party refused to stop. The spacecraft tumbled, the parachute failed, and Komarov was killed. He had demanded an open casket at his funeral — *"his revenge"* against the system that killed him. All that was left was a charred lump.
- **Lin Biao**, head of the People's Army and Mao's chosen successor, was tricked by Mao into plotting a coup. When the plot was discovered, Lin Biao fled in a plane. It crashed in Mongolia — the wreckage was found *"scattered across the steppe."*
- **Julia Grant**, a trans woman in northern England, fought the medical establishment to get surgery. Her psychiatrist refused her because she was *"not ladylike enough and too pushy."* After finally getting private surgery, complications destroyed her sex life, her partner left, and she was alone: *"If love slapped me in the face, I wouldn't even recognise it now."*
- **Richard Hofstadter** argued that American paranoia wasn't new — it was built into the country from its founding. The first settlers fled European corruption but brought their suspicious minds with them. Alone in the vast wilderness, they imagined dark conspiracies in Washington. The first was the **Bavarian Illuminati** conspiracy in the early 19th century. *"The paranoia in the suburbs is just part of a much larger darkness built into the very structure of America itself."*
- **Sandra Paul**, a successful model, married **Robin Douglas-Home** (nephew of a Prime Minister). He insisted she stop modelling and stay in the country while he went to London alone. When she wanted to know what he was doing: *"He thought that I shouldn't have to know everything about his life."* She eventually escaped — *"I had to be ruthless in order to be free."* He was later caught with the Marchioness of Londonderry and committed suicide.
- Political scientist **Peter Mair** argued that democracy didn't collapse — it was quietly abandoned. In the 1990s, politicians *"literally changed their roles"* from representing people to managing on behalf of a new technocratic class. *"It still looked like they were powerful and had control over events. But now the people had gone, beneath them was a void."*
- **Tony Martin**, a Norfolk farmer who shot and killed a burglar, became a lightning rod for a rage that Blair couldn't understand. Blair wrote: *"We have lost touch."* But Martin's uncle had been a leading member of the **League of Empire Loyalists**, who believed bankers and communists were conspiring to destroy the Empire — linking Brexit-era rural anger directly to post-imperial conspiracy.
- In Russia, **Boris Yeltsin's** "shock therapy" — the sudden introduction of free-market capitalism — created chaos. The oligarchs took over all the media and blocked opposition. Yeltsin was *"drunk most of the time."* Into this void stepped **Eduard Limonov**, who formed the **National Bolsheviks** — *"a fusion of fascism and communism"* — to shock people out of accepting corruption. The third member was musician **Yegor Letov**, who had led opposition to communism in the 1980s and now sang about Russia being *"trapped in a frozen world, buried under a mass of meaningless broken fragments from the past."*
- **Fareed Zakaria** posed the question the technocrats were all thinking: *"The people, we are told, are the most important. We are driven by the phrase, 'the American people are not stupid' — but what if they are?"*
- In 1978, Deng allowed the **Democracy Wall** in Beijing. An electrician at the zoo called **Wei Jingsheng** posted a message calling Deng *"a new fascist dictator."* Deng shut the wall down and arrested Wei. The brief experiment in free expression was over — Deng would allow economic freedom but never political freedom.
- **Bernard Kouchner** founded **Médecins Sans Frontières** after the Biafra crisis. His vision — *"there is no leftist and rightist suffering"* — was attacked from both sides. **Joan Baez** supported him publicly and was accused by **Jane Fonda** of being *"an unwitting agent of American imperialism."* Kouchner's rescue of Vietnamese boat people undermined the Left's self-belief and opened the door for a new kind of intervention that would eventually lead to Iraq.
- **Bob Geldof's Live Aid** raised millions for Ethiopian famine relief — but **Colonel Mengistu** used the food as a weapon, forcing mass relocations that **killed 100,000 people**. MSF estimated that *"the aid may have led to as many deaths as it had saved."* Geldof was horrified when he discovered the regime was using his charity. **Chai Ling**, the Tiananmen student leader, had studied **Maslow's self-actualisation** and told a journalist she was *"hoping for bloodshed"* — then fled. Curtis frames both as moments when idealism became complicit in violence.
- **David Graeber**, the anthropologist and activist who died in 2020, described *"the ultimate hidden truth of the world"* — a forgotten idea about collective action waiting to be rediscovered. Curtis uses his words to end the series as a call to imagine genuinely new futures.
- Curtis's throughline: individualism promised liberation but delivered isolation. The systems that were supposed to serve individuals instead learned to manage them. *"You were free but you were alone."*

## Most Controversial Claims

### 1. The West's dream world is funded by Chinese money
Curtis argues that China deliberately bought up American government debt to create a "safe bubble" around the US, keeping interest rates low so Americans would borrow and buy Chinese goods. This bubble insulated Americans from the Iraq War (no economic impact, no protests) and created *"the biggest consumer and property boom ever in history"* — a *"protected dream world increasingly detached from reality."*

### 2. Individualism is a form of control, not liberation
The entire series argues that the rise of individualism — sold as freedom — actually made people easier to manage. By atomising them, it destroyed the collective power that had allowed people to challenge elites. *"You were free but you were alone"* — and alone, you are weak.

### 3. Psychology's foundational experiments may be unreliable
Curtis highlights the **replication crisis**: when researchers tried to repeat key priming experiments, they repeatedly failed. Even Daniel Kahneman wrote an open letter warning of *"a train wreck looming."* The implication: much of what we believe about unconscious manipulation (nudging, priming, Cambridge Analytica) may be built on shaky foundations.

### 4. Conspiracy theories serve the powerful, not the powerless
Both Thornley and Tupac independently concluded: *"Suspicion is just another form of control."* Curtis argues that conspiracy theories — whether QAnon, Russiagate, or Illuminati stories — keep people distracted from the real structures of power. After the 2008 crash, *"nothing actually changed. The structure of power, the inequalities and the decay all carried on unchecked... while all kinds of groups who wanted to cling on to power protected themselves behind this brittle shell of conspiracy theories."*

### 5. Liberal Russia-panic was as irrational as QAnon
Curtis parallels the liberal conspiracy theory about Putin orchestrating Brexit and Trump with QAnon's beliefs, arguing both were ways of avoiding uncomfortable truths. The liberal opposition *"became lost in an endless conspiracy theory"* while *"Vladimir Putin, whose power in reality was becoming increasingly fragile at home, became, in the eyes of the West, a dark malevolent force, which made him seem far stronger than he really was."*

### 6. Democracy was deliberately dismantled from within
Curtis, citing political scientist **Peter Mair**, argues that Western politicians *"literally changed their roles"* in the 1990s — from representing people to becoming agents of a new technocratic bureaucracy. EU technocrat **Giandomenico Majone** explicitly designed *"non-majoritarian institutions"* that were *"by design, not directly accountable to voters or to their elected representatives."*

### 7. The 2008 crash was caused by giving up human judgment to algorithms
Curtis argues that bankers, rating agencies, and politicians all *"gave themselves up to this new way of thinking — a way of thinking that said that the data and the algorithms understood the complexity of the world better than you did... which meant that none of them saw the absurdity of what was really happening."*

### 8. Britain and America never faced their imperial past — and it keeps returning
The foundational argument of the series: both countries built *"dreamlike myths about their exceptionalism"* to shield themselves from the violence of empire. These myths — Cecil Sharp's folk England, American exceptionalism, the KKK's Walter Scott fantasies — keep resurfacing (Brexit nostalgia, Trump's "Make America Great Again") because the underlying guilt and fear were never resolved.

### 9. ISIS was the ultimate consequence of a romantic English dream
The chain from Cecil Sharp's folk revival → Gertrude Bell's fantasy Iraq → the CIA backing Saddam → the 2003 invasion → the Awakening tribes → ISIS is one of Curtis's boldest causal arguments. *"What had begun a long time ago as a make-believe version of England created in the deserts of Mesopotamia as the British Empire fell apart, had now turned into a terrifying nightmare."*

### 10. The machines don't need to understand us to control us
Perhaps Curtis's most unsettling claim: the shift from Google giving people information to Google taking people's data represents a fundamental change in power. The machines don't care about meaning, stories, or consciousness — they just need behaviour patterns. *"Consciousness was being sidelined."* Between Skinner's pigeons and China's social credit, the end point is a world where what you think and feel is irrelevant — only your observed behaviour counts.
